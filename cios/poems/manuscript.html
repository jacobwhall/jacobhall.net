I have been told of striped bass,
and know the circles which rise from this water at dawn
    are from those big fish feeding on flies.

    I have been told of stars,
and know the ones reflected here are the same
    you see, wherever you are.

    If you are up this late again,
you should know these are also the ones we saw
    on the balcony that night last May,

    when you borrowed my flannel and
we watched the lights flicker off on the building
    opposite ours, the brackish din of the party

    flowing away as you talked about
the play you were meaning to write. I wonder
    if you ever did begin it.

    Most of us, you said, are failed
messiahs. Our manuscripts thicken like the waists
    of American dads, our voices drift

    over the windless air and everyone hears
but nobody listens. We're in the same crisis
    transposed two years, the same circles,

    the same stars—bowls ashed, pupils
dilated, myths debunked, the dynamo stopped
    and the roman candles burnt to a nub.

    But haven't you always been
the one who still believed? The one who
    loved to dance with eyes closed,

    who dipped his feet in the fountain
outside the art museum (ignoring the sign that
    said you shouldn't) who asked me if

    I thought the wings of angels were more like
those of doves or those of dragonflies, and which
    I would prefer, if either, or neither—

    the last time we saw each other, your eyes
dodged mine and I wanted to ask what was wrong, but
    I never got a moment alone with you:

    so often, you told me, we ask our little
grand questions and end up sounding like idiots
    or worse—so often, you said, we end up

    staring at ceilings instead of skies—
and when I told you that line was good enough to steal,
    you said you already knew I'd take it.
